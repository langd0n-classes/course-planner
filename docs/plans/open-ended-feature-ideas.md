## Ideas I’d actually build

### 1. Persistent planning branches

**What it is:** Generalize the current single-session cancellation preview into named, persistent scenarios. An instructor could create “Exam moved one week,” “Two snow days,” or “Condensed 12-week term,” make several tentative changes, compare the branch against the live plan, and apply either the whole branch or selected changes.

**Problem solved:** Real schedule disruption is rarely one isolated cancellation. Several moves and redistributions interact, but the current what-if model evaluates one session at a time. Instructors need to reason about a coherent alternate semester, leave it overnight, discuss it, and return later.

**Design fit:** This is the fullest expression of “what-if before commit.” It also strengthens the app-as-workspace principle because scenarios remain inside the course rather than becoming spreadsheets or screenshots. Mock AI should stay optional: it could populate a branch with a realistic proposal, but the branching workflow is valuable without AI.

**Size:** Large.

**Human decisions:**

- Whether scenarios are private drafts or shareable
- Whether applying a branch is atomic
- How conflicts are handled if the live plan changes meanwhile
- Whether branching is limited to schedule and coverage initially or includes all course content

### 2. A course-memory loop, not just historical copies

**What it is:** Give each session, assessment, and module a structured post-use reflection: what actually happened, timing variance, student difficulties, successful activities, materials worth retaining, and a recommendation to keep, revise, or retire it. When cloning a term, surface these observations beside the new plan as an adaptation inbox.

**Problem solved:** `priorArt` and `clonedFromId` are currently written during cloning but barely consumed. They preserve ancestry without capturing why something should change. Instructors therefore repeat the same reconstruction work each semester.

This would make cloning a guided workflow:

1. Map the new institutional calendar.
2. Review prior sessions in context.
3. Accept, revise, merge, or omit each item.
4. Resolve capacity and coverage gaps created by the new calendar.
5. Record why important changes were made.

**Design fit:** Strongly supports the app as the enduring workspace. It is more specific and useful than the roadmap’s broad “past-semester content reference” and “history” headings: the important unit is actionable teaching memory, not merely a diff or audit trail.

It needs no real AI. Mock AI could demonstrate suggested adaptations from structured reflections without becoming authoritative.

**Size:** Large.

**Human decisions:**

- Which reflection fields should be structured versus free text
- Whether observations may include sensitive student information
- Whether reflection happens continuously or through an end-of-term review
- How much inherited material should appear automatically in a cloned term

### 3. Capacity and workload budgets

**What it is:** Add estimated and observed effort to sessions, activities, and assessments:

- Student preparation and completion time
- In-class minutes
- Instructor preparation and grading load
- Cognitive intensity or difficulty
- Modality and accessibility constraints

Show weekly load bands and flag overloaded weeks, assessment pileups, implausibly dense sessions, and redistributions that technically preserve I/P/A coverage but produce an unusable plan.

**Problem solved:** The current health model can call a course structurally sound while three major assessments land together or a canceled lecture’s content is moved into a session with no available time. Coverage is necessary, but it is not capacity.

**Design fit:** This extends “gaps are more important than coverage” into feasibility gaps. It also improves what-if analysis: a redistribution should answer not only “Does ordering remain valid?” but “Can this session absorb the work?”

It stretches the current architecture because health is presently almost entirely count-based. That is a worthwhile stretch. No export or real AI is required.

**Size:** Medium for basic estimates and weekly warnings; large if observed workload and accessibility constraints are included.

**Human decisions:**

- Which workload dimensions matter enough to model
- Whether estimates are instructor-authored, institution defaults, or both
- Whether warnings are advisory or can block applying a scenario
- How to represent asynchronous, hybrid, and multi-meeting sessions generically

### 4. Evidence-backed alignment

**What it is:** Replace the implicit assumption that an I/P/A label proves alignment with a traceable chain:

`learning objective → skill → learning activity → assessment → rubric criterion`

A coverage entry could carry a brief rationale or evidence reference. The workspace would then distinguish:

- Declared coverage
- Coverage supported by an actual activity
- Assessment linkage without a matching rubric criterion
- Objectives that map to no skills
- Skills assessed more heavily or lightly than intended

**Problem solved:** Today, adding an `assessed` coverage badge and linking a skill to an assessment can make the matrix look healthy even if the assessment does not meaningfully elicit that skill. The system measures bookkeeping completeness, not alignment quality.

**Design fit:** This deliberately tightens the meaning of “coverage” and makes gaps more honest. The primary experience should remain an interactive alignment view, not an accreditation export. If an institution eventually requires an alignment report, that export would still be a legitimate failure-state integration.

Mock AI could generate sample rationales or flag suspicious mappings, but instructors should remain the authority. I would not wait for real AI to build the underlying workflow.

**Size:** Large.

**Human decisions:**

- Whether rubric criteria become first-class entities
- How much evidence is required before coverage counts as verified
- Whether learning objectives are term-, course-, or module-level
- Whether the app supports configurable taxonomies beyond I/P/A

### 5. Make prerequisite readiness operational

**What it is:** Turn the existing `Skill.prerequisites` field into a real dependency graph. Show where a session relies on skills that have not yet been introduced or sufficiently practiced, identify bottleneck skills, and include downstream readiness failures in move/cancellation simulations.

For example, canceling an early session might leave its own coverage totals mostly intact while making four later sessions pedagogically premature. That is the more important impact.

**Problem solved:** The current validation understands I → P → A within each skill, but not relationships between skills. The schema already stores prerequisites, yet the planning logic and UI largely ignore them.

**Design fit:** This deepens the signature “skills flow through the semester” principle instead of adding a separate feature family. It makes broken lines meaningful across skills, not only within one skill’s three coverage levels. It should complement the planned flow visualization rather than duplicate it.

Mock AI is unnecessary; graph traversal and readiness rules should be deterministic.

**Size:** Medium to large.

**Human decisions:**

- Whether prerequisites are strict, recommended, or weighted
- What level counts as “ready”—introduced, practiced, or configurable
- How cycles and intentionally concurrent instruction are represented
- Whether readiness warnings are global or session-specific

## Architecture issues I would address first

Two existing modeling choices will undermine several future workflows:

- **Temporal ordering is not truly temporal.** Coverage validation sorts by module sequence, then session sequence, and uses the date only as a tiebreaker. A session can therefore move to a different date without changing its effective pedagogical order unless its sequence also changes. Calendar position should become the primary ordering truth for scheduled sessions, with explicit sequence used for undated planning and same-day ordering.

- **Skill ownership and cloning are inconsistent.** Term imports create term-scoped skills, but cloning reuses every coverage and assessment skill ID. A cloned term can therefore point to skills owned by the source term. The app needs an explicit policy: clone term-scoped skills with an origin link, promote them to course-level definitions, or model reusable skill definitions separately from term-specific adoption.

I would resolve those semantics before building richer history, branching, or dependency analysis.
